Module 2: The Verb "To Be", Nouns & Gender

Understand how Darija expresses "to be" for descriptions and identity, and learn about noun gender.

The "Zero Copula" - Expressing "To Be"

One of the first things to grasp in Darija is that for many simple "Subject + is/am/are + Noun/Adjective" sentences, there's no direct verb for "is/am/are." This is called the "zero copula." You simply put the subject and the noun/adjective together.

Identity (Subject + Noun)

English Darija Script Transcription Audio
I am a student (male). أنا طالب Ana talib.
She is a teacher (female). هي أستاذة Hiya ustada.
You are Moroccan (male). نتا مغربي Nta mghribi.
He is a doctor. هو طبيب Huwa tbib.
Remember Pronouns: You learned subject pronouns in Module 1: أنا (Ana - I), نتا (Nta - You m.), نتي (Nti - You f.), هو (Huwa - He), هي (Hiya - She).

Description (Subject + Adjective)

Adjectives will be covered in more detail later, but here are some examples of how the zero copula works with them. Notice how adjectives might change for gender.

English Darija Script Transcription Audio
I am happy (male speaking). أنا فرحان Ana fr7an.
She is happy. هي فرحانة Hiya fr7ana.
The book is big. الكتاب كبير L-ktab kbir.
The car is small. الطموبيل صغيرة Ttomobil sghira.
Important: While the zero copula is common, there *are* ways to express "to be" for location (using `kayen/f`) or for emphasis/existence in certain contexts, which you'll see in later modules (e.g., Module 11 "Existential Expressions").

Nouns and Gender

In Darija, like many Romance languages, nouns have a grammatical gender: either masculine or feminine. There is no neuter gender. Knowing the gender of a noun is important because adjectives and sometimes verbs need to agree with it.

Identifying Feminine Nouns

A common (but not absolute) rule is that **feminine nouns often end in an "-a" sound.**

English Noun Darija Script Transcription Gender Audio
Car طموبيل(ة) ttomobil(a) Feminine
Table طبلة tbla Feminine
School مدرسة mdrasa Feminine
Girl / Daughter بنت bent Feminine (exception - doesn't end in -a)
The `(ة)` in `طموبيلة` (ttomobila) is the Arabic letter "ta marbuta," which often indicates feminine nouns and is pronounced as an "-a" sound at the end of a word if not followed by another word in a construct state. In Darija transcription, we usually just write the "-a".

Identifying Masculine Nouns

Nouns that do **not** end in "-a" are often (but not always) masculine.

English Noun Darija Script Transcription Gender Audio
Book كتاب ktab Masculine
Man / Husband راجل rajl Masculine
Boy / Son ولد weld Masculine
House دار dar Feminine (exception - often treated as feminine despite no -a ending)
Exceptions are Key! There are exceptions to these gender rules (like `bent` and `dar` above). The best way to learn noun gender is to memorize it with each new word. When in doubt, listen to how native speakers use adjectives with the noun.

The Definite Article "l-" (The)

To say "the" in Darija, you often add the prefix "l-" (or "el-") to the beginning of the noun. The exact form can change slightly depending on the first letter of the noun (due to "sun" and "moon" letters from Arabic, but for A1, "l-" is a good starting point).

English Darija Script (Indefinite) Transcription (Indefinite) Darija Script (Definite) Transcription (Definite) Audio (Definite)
A book / The book كتاب ktab الكتاب l-ktab
A car / The car طموبيل ttomobil الطموبيل t-ttomobil / l-ttomobil
A man / The man راجل rajl الراجل r-rajl / l-rajl
Sun and Moon Letters: If a noun starts with a "sun letter" (like t, d, r, s, sh, n, l, etc.), the "l" of the definite article assimilates and you pronounce the first letter of the noun doubled. For example, شمس (shms - sun) becomes الشمس (ash-shms). For "moon letters" (like b, m, k, q, 3, etc.), the "l" is pronounced. For example, قمر (qamar - moon) becomes القمر (l-qamar). Don't worry too much about memorizing these rules now; your ear will pick it up with exposure.
