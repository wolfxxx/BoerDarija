A2 Module 13: Relative Clauses with `lli`

Learn to connect ideas and add more information about nouns using the relative pronoun `lli` (that/which/who).

What is a Relative Clause?

A relative clause is a part of a sentence that gives more information about a noun. In English, we often use words like "who," "which," or "that" to introduce these clauses.

Example: "I saw the man who lives next door." The bold part is the relative clause, describing "the man."

In Darija, the most common word to introduce these clauses is `lli` (اللي).

Using `lli` (اللي) - That/Which/Who

`lli` is very versatile and can refer to people, things, or places. It usually follows the noun it's describing.

Basic Structure: **[Main Clause with Noun] + `lli` + [Clause describing the Noun]**

Referring to People (`lli` = who/whom)

English Darija (Script) Transcription Breakdown Audio
The man who came yesterday. الراجل اللي جا البارح. R-rajl lli ja l-bare7. Man + who + came yesterday
The woman who works here. المرا اللي كاتخدم هنا. L-mra lli katkhdem hna. Woman + who + works here
I saw the students who passed the exam. شفت التلامذ اللي نجحو ف الامتحان. Sheft t-tlamed lli nje7u f l-imti7an. I saw students + who + passed in the exam

Referring to Things (`lli` = that/which)

English Darija (Script) Transcription Breakdown Audio
The book that is on the table. الكتاب اللي على الطبلة. L-ktab lli 3la t-tbla. Book + that + is on the table
The car (f.) that I bought. الطموبيل اللي شريت. T-ttomobil lli shrit. Car + that + I bought
I want the tagine that has meat. بغيت الطاجين اللي فيه اللحم. Bghit t-tajin lli fih l-l7m. I want tagine + that + has meat in it
Definite Nouns: `lli` is typically used when the noun it refers to is definite (i.e., has "the" - `l-`). For indefinite nouns ("a man who..."), the structure can sometimes be different or `lli` might be omitted, but for A2, focusing on `lli` with definite nouns is a good start.

More Examples and Variations

`lli` as the Subject of the Relative Clause

In the examples above, `lli` often acts as the subject of the verb in the relative clause (e.g., "the man `lli` ja" - the man *who came*).

`lli` referring to the Object within the Relative Clause

Sometimes the noun described is the *object* of the verb in the relative clause. In such cases, a pronoun suffix might be needed on the verb in the relative clause to refer back to `lli` (and thus the main noun). This is more advanced, but here's a taste:

  • The book that I read: الكتاب اللي قريتو. (L-ktab lli qrit-u.)
    Here, `-u` (it/him) refers back to `l-ktab`. Lit. "The book that I read it."
  • The woman whom I saw: المرا اللي شفتها. (L-mra lli sheft-ha.)
    Here, `-ha` (her/it) refers back to `l-mra`. Lit. "The woman that I saw her."
For A2 Focus: Don't worry too much about the object pronoun suffix within the `lli` clause yet if it's confusing. Focus on understanding and forming simpler sentences where `lli` is the subject of the following verb, or where the verb naturally makes sense without a resumptive pronoun (like "L-ktab lli 3la t-tbla").

Practice Time!

Exercise 1: Combine the Sentences

Combine the two sentences into one using `lli`.

  1. I saw the man. The man was tall.
    Darija: Sheft r-rajl lli kan twil.
  2. This is the car. I bought the car yesterday.
    Darija: Hadi t-ttomobil lli shrit l-bare7.
  3. The students came. The students are smart.
    Darija: T-tlamed lli jaw dkiyin. (jaw = they came; dkiyin = smart pl.)

Exercise 2: Translate to Darija

Translate these sentences using `lli`.

  1. The house that is big is beautiful. D-dar lli kbira zwina.
  2. I want the tea that has mint. Bghit atay lli fih n-n3na3.
  3. The teacher (f.) who teaches me is kind. L-ustada lli katqrrini Drifa. (`katqrrini` = she teaches me)
  4. Where is the book that was here? Fin l-ktab lli kan hna?
Using `lli` allows you to make your sentences more descriptive and natural. Try to find opportunities to use it when describing people or things!
