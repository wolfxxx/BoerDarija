A2 Module 14: Simple Conditional "If" with `Ila` / `Ida`

Learn how to express conditions and possible outcomes using "if" in Darija.

Introducing Conditional Sentences

Conditional sentences talk about a condition (the "if" part) and a result or consequence (the "then" part, which is often implied). We use them to discuss possibilities, hypothetical situations, or cause and effect.

In Darija, the most common word for "if" is `ila` (إلى) or `ida` (إذا). `Ila` is generally more common in everyday speech.

Structure 1: `Ila` + Past Tense, Result in Future Tense

This is a very common structure for talking about real, possible future conditions and their likely results.

Pattern: **`Ila` + [Verb in Past Tense], `ghadi` + [Verb in Present Stem/Form]**

(If [condition happened/is met], then [result will happen])

English Darija (Script) Transcription Breakdown Audio
If I go to the market, I will buy vegetables. إلى مشيت للسوق، غادي نشري الخضرة. Ila mshit l s-souq, ghadi nshri l-khdra. If I went (past), I will buy (future)
If she studies, she will pass the exam. إلى قرات، غادي تنجح ف الامتحان. Ila qrat, ghadi tnje7 f l-imti7an. If she read/studied (past), she will pass (future)
If you (m.s.) have time, we will go to the cinema. إلى كان عندك الوقت، غادي نمشيو للسينما. Ila kan 3ndek l-weqt, ghadi nmshiw l s-sinima. If you had (past) time, we will go (future)
"Past" Tense for Future Condition: It might seem strange to use the past tense in the "if" clause for a future possibility. This is a common feature in many languages for this type of conditional (sometimes called "first conditional" or "real future conditional"). Think of it as "If [this condition *were to be* met]..."

Structure 2: `Ila` + Present Tense (`ka-`), Result in Present/Future

This structure can be used for general truths, habitual conditions, or sometimes also for future possibilities, similar to Structure 1.

Pattern: **`Ila` + [Verb with `ka-`], [Result with `ka-` (habitual) or `ghadi` (future)]**

English Darija (Script) Transcription Breakdown Audio
If I eat a lot, I get tired. (Habitual) إلى كاناكل بزاف، كانعيا. Ila kanakul bzzaf, kan3ya. If I eat (present `ka-`), I get tired (present `ka-`)
If you (f.s.) study, you will understand. (Future possibility) إلى كاتقراي، غادي تفهمي. Ila katqray, ghadi tfhmi. If you study (present `ka-`), you will understand (future)
For A2, the **`Ila` + Past Tense, Future Tense** structure (Structure 1) is often the most straightforward and commonly taught for future possibilities. Structure 2 is also valid but can have more nuances.

Order of Clauses

The "if" clause (`ila` clause) can come before or after the main result clause. If it comes after, you typically don't need a comma (or pause in speech).

Example: غادي نشري الخضرة إلى مشيت للسوق.
Ghadi nshri l-khdra ila mshit l s-souq.
(I will buy vegetables if I go to the market.)

Negative Conditions

To say "If... not...", you negate the verb in the `ila` clause.

Example: If I don't study, I will not pass.
إلى ما قريتش، ما غاديش ننجح.
Ila ma qrit-sh, ma ghadi-sh nnje7.
(Here `qrit-sh` is "I did not study" - past tense negation)

Example: If it doesn't rain (الشتا ما طاحتش - shta ma ta7tsh - rain didn't fall) tomorrow, we will go out.
إلى الشتا ما طاحتش غدا، غادي نخرجو.
Ila sh-shta ma ta7tsh ghdda, ghadi nkhurju.

Practice Time!

Exercise 1: Complete the Conditional

Fill in the blanks to complete the conditional sentences. Use Structure 1 (`Ila` + Past, Result with `ghadi`).

  1. إلى dert (dert - I did) الرياضة (riyada - sports)، ghadi nkun (nkun - I will be) مزيان.
  2. إلى kan (kan - he was) البرد (l-berd - cold)، ghadi nlbs (nlbs - I will wear) جاكيطة (jakita - jacket).
  3. إلى shrebti (shrebti - you drank) بزاف د القهوة، ma ghadi-sh tn3s (ma ghadi-sh tn3s - you will not sleep) مزيان.

Exercise 2: Translate to Darija

Translate these sentences using simple conditionals.

  1. If I have money, I will travel to Fes.
    Answer: Ila kan 3ndi l-flus, ghadi nsafr l Fas.
  2. If she comes early, she will find us here. (to find: `lqa`)
    Answer: Ila jat bekri, ghadi tlqana hna. (jat = she came; tlqana = she will find us)
  3. We will eat tagine if you (m.s.) cook it.
    Answer: Ghadi naklu ttajin ila tiyyebtih. (tiyyebtih = you cooked it)
Conditional sentences are very useful for planning and discussing possibilities. Try making up some "if... then..." scenarios about your day!
