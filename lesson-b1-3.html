B1 Module 3: Active & Passive Participles

Learn how to form and use active and passive participles, which can function as adjectives or parts of verb phrases.

What are Participles?

Participles are verb forms that can act like adjectives (describing nouns) or be used to form certain verb tenses or voices (like the passive voice).

  • **Active Participle:** Describes something or someone *doing* the action of the verb (e.g., "a *writing* man," "the *running* boy").
  • **Passive Participle:** Describes something or someone that *has had the action of the verb done to it* (e.g., "a *written* letter," "a *broken* chair").

In Darija, participles are often formed based on specific patterns from the verb root.

Active Participles (Pattern: `fa3el` فاعل)

For many basic three-letter verbs (Form I), the active participle often follows the pattern `CaCeC` (where C represents a root consonant). The feminine form adds an `-a`.

Verb Root (Meaning) Active Participle (Masc. - Script/Trans.) Active Participle (Fem. - Script/Trans.) Meaning Audio (Masc/Fem)
kteb (to write) كاتب / kateb كاتبة / katba writing (one who writes)
khdem (to work) خدام / khddam خدامة / khddama working (one who works)
jles (to sit) جالس / gales گالسة / galsa sitting (one who is sitting)
wqef (to stand) واقف / waqef واقفة / waqfa standing (one who is standing)
na3es (to sleep) ناعس / na3es ناعسة / na3sa sleeping (one who is sleeping)

Example: الراجل الكاتب. (R-rajl l-kateb.) - The writing man / The writer.

Example: هي خدامة مزيان. (Hiya khddama mzyan.) - She is working well / She is a good worker.

Example: لقيتو ناعس. (Lqitu na3es.) - I found him sleeping. (`lqitu` = I found him)

Active participles often describe a state resulting from an action or someone who habitually does an action. They function very much like adjectives and must agree in gender and number. Plural is often formed by adding `-in` (e.g., `katbin`, `khddamin`).

Passive Participles (Pattern: `mf3ul` مفعول)

For many basic three-letter verbs, the passive participle often follows the pattern `m-C-CuC`. This describes something that has had the action done to it. The feminine form adds an `-a`.

Verb Root (Meaning) Passive Participle (Masc. - Script/Trans.) Passive Participle (Fem. - Script/Trans.) Meaning Audio (Masc/Fem)
kteb (to write) مكتوب / mektub مكتوبة / mektuba written
fhem (to understand) مفهوم / mfhum مفهومة / mfhuma understood
feta7 (to open) مفتوح / meftu7 مفتوحة / meftu7a opened
shedd (to close) مسدود / mesdud مسدودة / mesduda closed
hres (to break) مهزوز / mherres (common) or مهرس مهروزة / mherrsa broken

Example: الباب مفتوح. (L-bab meftu7.) - The door is open(ed).

Example: الرسالة مكتوبة بالدارجة. (R-risala mektuba b d-darija.) - The letter is written in Darija.

Using Passive Participles for Simple Passive Voice

Passive participles are key to forming simple passive voice sentences. This is where the subject of the sentence receives the action, rather than doing it.

Structure: **[Noun (receiver of action)] + [Conjugated `kan` (to be) - often omitted in present] + [Passive Participle (agrees with noun)]**

Present Passive (often without `kan`):

  • The door is closed: الباب مسدود. (L-bab mesdud.)
  • The food (f.) is cooked: الماكلة مطيوبة. (L-makla mtiyyba.) (from `tiyyeb` - to cook)
  • The lesson (m.) is understood: الدرس مفهوم. (D-ders mfhum.)

Past Passive (with `kan` in past tense):

  • The door was closed: الباب كان مسدود. (L-bab kan mesdud.)
  • The letter (f.) was written: البرا كانت مكتوبة. (L-bra kanet mektuba.)
This is a simple way to form the passive. More complex passive constructions exist but this is a great starting point for B1. The participle acts like an adjective describing the state of the noun.

Give the masculine singular active participle for these verbs:

  1. `shrb` (to drink): shareb
  2. `rje3` (to return): raje3
  3. `dkhel` (to enter): dakhel

  3. `shel` (to light/turn on): mesh3ul (lit/turned on)

Exercise 2: Active or Passive?

Translate these sentences to Darija, using the correct participle form.

  1. The man is standing near the door. (waqef)
    Answer: R-rajl waqef 7da l-bab.
  2. The window (shrjem - m.) is broken. (mherres)
    Answer: Sh-shrjem mherres.
  3. She is sleeping in the room. (na3sa)
    Answer: Hiya na3sa f l-bit.
  4. This lesson (ders - m.) is written well. (mektub)
    Answer: Had d-ders mektub mzyan.
Participles are very useful for describing states and forming passive sentences. Listen for the `CaCeC` (active) and `m-C-CuC` (passive) patterns in everyday speech!
