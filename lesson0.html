Module 0: Darija Sounds & Transcription

Welcome to your first step in learning Moroccan Darija! Understanding how Darija sounds are pronounced and written is crucial for your learning journey.

Why is This Important?

Moroccan Darija has some sounds that don't exist in English. The transcription system used on this site (and commonly by learners) uses Latin letters, including some numbers, to represent these unique sounds. Mastering these will help you:

  • Pronounce words correctly.
  • Understand native speakers better.
  • Read and write Darija in its transcribed form.

Listen Carefully! The best way to learn pronunciation is by listening to native speakers. Pay close attention to the audio examples that will be provided. Don't be afraid to repeat the sounds out loud.

Special Consonants (Represented by Numbers)

These are some of the most distinctive sounds in Darija, often represented by numbers in informal transcription because there's no direct Latin alphabet equivalent.

3 Play Sound

ع

This is the Arabic letter 'Ayn (ﻉ). It's a voiced pharyngeal fricative, meaning it's produced deep in the throat. It has no English equivalent.

How to try: Imagine you're trying to say "aaah" but constrict your throat muscles. It's a bit like the sound some people make when they're about to be sick, but more controlled and voiced.

Example: عافاك (3afak - please) Play Example

ح

This is the Arabic letter Ḥāʾ (ح). It's a voiceless pharyngeal fricative. It's like a very breathy 'h' sound, made further back in the throat than the English 'h'.

How to try: Imagine you've just eaten something very spicy and you're exhaling sharply, or like you're fogging up a mirror with a strong, breathy "haaa."

Example: سبعة (sb7a - seven) Play Example

9 Play Sound

ق

This is the Arabic letter Qāf (ق). It's a voiceless uvular stop. It's like a 'k' sound, but produced much further back in the throat, at the uvula.

How to try: Try to say 'k' but pull the back of your tongue as far back as it can go, touching the soft part at the very back of your mouth (uvula).

Example: قهوة (qhwa - coffee) Play Example

Vowels

Darija vowels can be a bit tricky as their pronunciation can vary. Generally, we distinguish between short and long vowels. For A1, focus on these basic sounds:

  • a: Like the 'a' in "cat" or sometimes "father" (e.g., باب bab - door).
  • i: Like the 'ee' in "see" (e.g., كبير kbir - big).
  • u: Like the 'oo' in "moon" (e.g., فلوس flus - money).

Sometimes 'e' or 'o' are used in transcriptions, often representing variations of 'i'/'a' or 'u' respectively, or influenced by French loanwords. The most important thing is to listen to native speakers.

Vowel Fluidity: You'll notice that short vowels are sometimes "swallowed" or very subtle in Darija, especially between consonants. This is normal!

Doubled Consonants (Shadda)

When you see a consonant doubled in transcription (e.g., 'mm', 'll', 'dda'), it means the sound is emphasized or held slightly longer. In Arabic script, this is marked with a symbol called "shadda" (ّ ) above the letter.

Example: مّي (mmi - my mom) vs. مي (mi - water - if it were written this way, but 'water' is 'ma'). The 'mm' in 'mmi' is held longer.

Another example: قطّ (qeṭṭ - cat, note the ṭ here is an emphatic T sound, but focus on the doubled t) has a stronger 'tt' sound than a single 't'.

Practice Tips

  • Listen, Listen, Listen: Find Darija music, watch videos, and (if possible) talk to native speakers. Your ear needs to get used to these sounds.
  • Record Yourself: Try saying the example words and compare your pronunciation to the (future) audio provided.
  • Don't Aim for Perfection Immediately: Some sounds take time. Focus on being understood.
  • Focus on the Casablanca Dialect: As you progress, you'll notice regional variations. This course focuses on the widely understood Casablanca pronunciation.
